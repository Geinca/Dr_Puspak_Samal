Total Shoulder Replacement is performed for patients with:

  • Severe glenohumeral osteoarthritis
  • Post-traumatic arthritis or fractures
  • Rheumatoid arthritis or other inflammatory conditions
  • Rotator cuff tear arthropathy (reverse TSA)
  • Failed previous shoulder surgeries

Clinical Decision Criteria:

  • Chronic shoulder pain not relieved by conservative management
  • Stiffness or loss of range of motion
  • Imaging showing joint space narrowing, osteophytes, or humeral head deformity

ðŸ”ª Surgical Technique

Approaches

  • Deltopectoral approach: Most commonly used
  • Superior approach: Used in specific cases
  • Reverse TSA: Preferred when rotator cuff is deficient

Implant Components

  • Humeral component: Metal stem with ball head
  • Glenoid component: Polyethylene socket (anatomic) or metal baseplate with glenosphere (reverse)
  • Fixation: Cemented or uncemented

Note: Reverse TSA is increasingly popular for elderly patients with cuff-deficient shoulders.
